What is psychedelic-assisted psychotherapy?

Psychedelic-assisted psychotherapy is an alternate, more natural method of treating mental illnesses as opposed to more mainstream types of care, such as the use of pharmaceutical antidepressants.

This treatment refers to the consumption of hallucinogenic drugs such as psilocybin mushrooms, LSD, MDMA and ayahuasca in order to assist the patient during their ongoing therapeutic counselling with a trained professional. It has been proven in many studies that the use of psychedelic substances in conjunction with psychotherapy can improve one’s mental health and general well-being by drastically improving their symptoms of various mental health disorders, leading them to experience greater mindfulness, and helping them to encounter healthier mental breakthroughs.

How does it work?

Psychedelic-induced therapy sessions have shown to increase the amount of connectivity patterns in the brain than during other conscious states. Moreover, the use of hallucinogenic drugs is associated with higher cognitive flexibility and heightened sense of self-awareness. In fact, psychedelic-induced brain connections are richer and more diverse than those in non-psychedelic states (Carhart-Harris, et al., par. 1). Depending on which mental illness you are fighting, different psychedelics are useful for different disorders. For example, MDMA is especially useful for PTSD patients as it helps them process and resolve their psychological trauma without experiencing any considerable damage to their mental state (Bui, et al., par. 14). To continue, LSD-assisted psychotherapy can lead to a significant decrease in common anxiety symptoms in the long term, even after only one session (par. 17), and psilocybin mushrooms most commonly leads to long term effects of mindfulness and serenity (Nichols, par. 39).

The future of medicine

Spreading awareness about the significance of this treatment on an international scale will bring light to alternative medicine and its countless benefits despite all of the previously misconceived truths about these drugs. Psychedelic substances should not be strictly seen as a recreational drug and should be considered as an official and effective treatment for mental illness in modern science. Indeed, psychedelics can lead its users to experience “positive long-term mental health consequences, rather than daily neurochemical corrections in brain dysfunctions” (Schenberg, par. 12). Many experts in the field truly believe that psychedelic medicine is the future of pharmaceuticals and mental health care, and there is so much more to discover than what we already do.
